Abstract

Abstract

We present HippoCamp, a new benchmark designed to evaluate agents' capabilities on multimodal file management. Unlike existing agent benchmarks that focus on tasks like web interaction, tool-use, or software automation in generic settings, HippoCamp evaluates agents in user-centric environments to model individual user profiles and search from massive personal files for context-aware reasoning. Our benchmark instantiates device-scale file systems over real-world profiles spanning diverse modalities, comprising 42.4 GB of data across over 2K real-world files. Building upon the raw files, we construct 581 QA pairs to assess agents' capabilities in search, evidence perception, and multi-step reasoning. To facilitate fine-grained analysis, we provide 46.1K densely annotated structured trajectories for step-wise failure diagnosis. We evaluate a wide range of state-of-the-art multimodal large language models and agentic methods on HippoCamp. Our comprehensive experiments reveal a significant performance gap: even the most advanced commercial models achieve merely a 48.0% accuracy in user profiling, struggling particularly with long-horizon retrieval and cross-modal reasoning within dense personal file systems. Furthermore, our step-wise failure diagnosis identifies multimodal perception and evidence grounding as the primary bottlenecks. Ultimately, HippoCamp exposes the critical limitations of current agents in realistic, user-centric environments and provides a robust foundation for developing next-generation personal AI assistants.

Overview

Personalized Multimodal Memory Benchmark

HippoCamp is a benchmark designed to evaluate agents' ability to search, perceive, and reason over long-term, realistic, large-scale personal file systems. Through tasks that demand grounded retrieval, cross-file pattern inference, and long-horizon reasoning, the benchmark rigorously evaluates the personalized multimodal memory of contextual agents.

Realistic Personal Environments

We faithfully simulate user-level digital ecosystems by constructing three distinct, file-intensive profiles. This design captures long-term continuity, idiosyncratic folder structures, and the interconnected nature of real-world personal file systems.

Device-Scale Corpus and Supervision

We provide a massive dataset comprising over 2000 heterogeneous files (totaling 42.4 GB), accompanied by 581 user-need-driven, evidence-grounded queries. To support rigorous evaluation, we include 46.1K fine-grained annotations.

Search, Perception, and Reasoning

We formulate a rigorous question-answering benchmark built on two core task categories: factual retention and profiling. These tasks require search, multimodal evidence perception, and personalized reasoning across interrelated files over time.

Verifiable Personalization Remains Hard

Strong systems still struggle with entity disambiguation, multimodal grounding, and iterative evidence synthesis, indicating that personalization remains far from solved.

Annotation Hierarchy

Hierarchical Annotation Schema

The pyramid organizes supervision from low-level atomic grounding and action traces to structured trajectories and QA tasks, with increasing abstraction and aggregation toward user-level memory. Profiling queries sit at the top, requiring long-horizon, cross-modal integration of multiple factual-retention facts into a coherent user model.

Profiles

Three Archetypal Personal Computing Environments

Each profile instantiates a distinct personal-device environment characterized along multiple dimensions, and is paired with representative file-content statistics and factual-retention and profiling QA examples.

Bei Weiwei

Profile A represents a student and content-creator context, highlighting multimodal daily-life traces, study materials, and creative-media artifacts.

Adam Turner

Profile B represents a legal-executive environment with recurrent coordination, administrative records, structured organization, and workflow-heavy professional documents.

Victoria Anne Clarke

Profile C represents a senior-financial-analyst setting dominated by large PDFs, structured artifacts, and long-horizon planning and reporting materials.

Tasks

From Single Facts To User-Level Profiles

HippoCamp formulates personalized file management as open-ended, evidence-grounded question answering. Factual retention requires retrieving and reasoning over verifiable file-grounded facts, whereas profiling synthesizes many grounded facts across time into coherent user-level inferences such as preferences, behavioral patterns, scheduling information, retrospective reflections, and workflows.

Search Perception Reasoning
Task Family 1

Factual Retention

A factual retention query requires producing a precise answer that is fully supported by file-grounded evidence under realistic filesystem "haystack" conditions. Evaluation emphasizes high-fidelity factual recall, structured alignment, and evidence-backed answers with minimal hallucination.

Example 1: Cross-Modal Asset Retrieval

The first example asks the agent to locate a previously written vlog script and identify the corresponding cat photos required by the script. This instance tests precise file localization, structured fact extraction from documents, and cross-modal matching under explicit constraints.

Example 2: Document-Video Compliance Verification

The second example evaluates rule-based factual verification under multimodal evidence. The query asks whether the "Saver Menu" logo placement in a user advertisement complies with McDonald's clearspace guidelines.

Task Family 2

Profiling

A profiling query requires inferring user-level attributes from device-resident evidence distributed across files, modalities, and time. Evaluation emphasizes profile consistency, correct temporal anchoring, executability of suggested actions when applicable, and traceability to grounded evidence.

Profiling Subtasks

Profiling queries are decomposed into five complementary subtasks: preferences, behavioral patterns, scheduling information, retrospective reflections, and workflows. These subtasks share the same evidence-grounding requirement but differ in the dominant abstraction operator, ranging from event-level reconstruction to trait-level generalization.

Profiling Atlas

Preferences

Preferences profiling captures stable, trait-like choices that generalize across situations and requires evidence-backed abstraction from episodic traces.
Results

Main Results on HippoCamp

We evaluate representative multimodal large language models and agent methods on profiling and factual retention, reporting F1 and accuracy for each archetypal profile and the overall average.

Autonomous Agent Systems

ChatGPT Agent Mode

ChatGPT Agent Mode performs best overall, achieving 21.0 F1 / 48.3 Acc on profiling and 35.3 F1 / 62.8 Acc on factual retention.

Search Agent Methods

Search-R1

Search-R1 attains strong factual-retention F1 on document-heavy profiles, but achieves only 10.8 overall profiling F1 with 5.0 Acc, indicating that locating candidate files alone is insufficient for user-level inference.

Capability-Wise Decomposition

Perception Remains a Bottleneck

Perception is a consistent bottleneck across families, with uniformly low accuracy. For example, Standard RAG achieves 13.8% and Search-R1 achieves 15.7% on profiling tasks.

Search Alone Is Not Enough

Search-centric agents attain strong search F1 on profiling, yet their reasoning accuracy remains much lower. Retrieval alone is insufficient for correct extraction.

Perception Remains Weak

Perception is a consistent bottleneck across families, underscoring the challenge of interpreting heterogeneous modalities in personal file systems.

ChatGPT Agent Mode Is Most Balanced

ChatGPT Agent Mode is the only system with comparatively balanced performance across search, perception, and reasoning, consistent with its iterative discovery advantage.

Analysis

Where Current Agents Fail

We complement the profile-wise results with finer-grained analysis. We decompose performance by agent capability, characterize systematic failure and success modes, and distill design principles for future agents.

Retrieval Mismatch

RAG-based systems often fail to distinguish user-relevant personal files from semantically related but contextually irrelevant documents.

Grounding Avoidance

Reasoning-centric agents can locate candidates yet avoid committing to evidence-grounded answers.

Hard Evidence Hallucination

Terminal-based agents operating in sandboxed environments frequently fabricate file paths or metadata.

Entity Misattribution

Even the strongest system can retrieve relevant evidence yet bind it to the wrong referent.

Difficulty Axis 1

Evidence Breadth

The number of ground-truth evidence files required per query is sharply peaked at a single evidence file yet exhibits a pronounced heavy tail. The long tail provides hard stress tests for robust retrieval and evidence management.

Difficulty Axis 2

Modality Breadth

While unimodal queries dominate, HippoCamp contains a meaningful multimodal tail, including two-modality queries and additional instances requiring three or more modalities. High-value assistance frequently hinges on aligning evidence across modalities.

Difficulty Axis 3

Reasoning Depth

HippoCamp concentrates around medium-depth problems while maintaining a non-trivial long tail of deep multi-step queries. Real personal-computing queries often require multiple rounds of retrieval, evidence inspection, cross-file reconciliation, and verification.

Difficulty

Difficulty Distribution

Across the full benchmark, difficulty is centered at a moderate-to-high level (mean 57.5, median 49.1, n=581) with a substantial hard tail: 26.7% of questions score at least 70. Profiling is systematically harder than factual retention (means 89.3 vs. 53.8).

Performance

Performance Declines with Difficulty

Average judge score generally decreases as difficulty increases. Even in low-to-mid difficulty bins, absolute scores remain modest for most methods, and in the high-difficulty regime several method families approach near-floor behavior.

Evaluation

Unified Evaluation Pipeline

The evaluator loads benchmark JSON records, dispatches them to a method backend through a shared interface, collects answers and optional traces, and computes standardized metrics for aggregation and analysis.
